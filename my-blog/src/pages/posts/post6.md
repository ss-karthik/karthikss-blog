## **Commute**
I have to say, this thought was conceived due to my increasing frustration with regards to the average commuting experience in a big city these days.  Note that I am writing from the viewpoint of Hyderabad, one of the big metros of India. There are other big metros that might be better or worse than this, though I believe the general essence I am trying to communicate broadly remains the same across most of them, especially in India. Residents of other cities that want to add more colour and cite differences are welcome to share their thoughts in my mailbox.
<br><br>

## **The Factors**
There are three main factors I have identified that decide your decision regarding the way you commute. Time, Cost, and Comfort. It is very hard to hit a sweet spot with all these 3. Instead you have to sacrifice at least one, or even two (if you're having an unlucky day) to prioritize the factor that you want. Let's see what I mean by each factor before I try and evaluate various commute options in relation to them.

- *Time*: Quicker the better. Commute for as less time as possible. Reach as fast as possible.<br>
- *Cost*: Cheaper the better. Spend as little money as possible on the daily commute.<br>
- *Comfort*: More comfortable the better. Refers not only to the comfort during the commute, but how exhausted one is after the commuting process.
<br><br>


## **Time**
So Let's start by minimizing commute time. The obvious first thought is using your own vehicle, a 2 or 4 wheeler. Generally, this would be quick as there would be no wait time and you decide when you start your commmute and which route you take. This could also be cheaper than other alternatives, though that is not really a guarantee as it relies on fuel prices. The downside here is that you give up comfort. The strain that the commute has on you is felt and you are not just a passenger, so the commute is definitely an involved activity.
<br><br>


## **Comfort**
Now, let's say we want to maximize comfort, while still reaching relatively quick and have a certain degree of freedom as to how you reach there. The other option then becomes to hire a cab or an auto-rickshaw. This will take you from A to B with relative comfort. This however is not as quick as your own vehicle, as there is a certain degree of wait involved. But the main downside is the Cost. It is markedly expensive to commute in this manner, and doing so in the modern metros on a daily basis is literally a luxury.
<br><br>

## **Cost**
The third alternative is to minimmize the cost. This means using Public Transport. For readers from a few countries, this might be a no brainer and generally their default method of commuting as it might be the sweet spot across all 3 factors for them. However, let me tell you, it is not as simple as that here. There are mainly two public transport options in Hyderabad: The City Bus service and the Metro. The Metro Network is not as extensive as the Bus Network and only connects major areas, which means you need an additonal commute from the Metro Station to wherever you need to get to. If you're lucky enough such that the places you need to go to are available on the metro network, then it is the best option. Though pricier than the City Bus, it is still less expensive compared to private transport. Unfortunately, this is not a common scenario and does not apply to me like most people.

This leaves us with the Bus. The Bus is for sure extensive, and is definitely the cheapest way to commute around the city (cheaper than the metro as well). But what does minimizing the cost affect? The first and most notable hit is the increase in commute time. Now you are no longer in control of how you get to your destination. Since the Bus must cater to many areas, it generally takes a longer way around and the route passes through the main roads, which most definitely will have maximum traffic. The frequency of buses also varies, so depending on where you're planning to go, the wait times are also an added increase in the commute time. This also affects comfort in a big way. If you're lucky such that you board the bus at the first stop and get down at the last stop, without requiring changes in between, then you may find a seat to sit. Otherwise, standing for the entire duration of the commute in a crowded bus is not uncommon at all. And though the bus serves more places than the Metro, there might be an average walk of upto a kilometer, to reach your final destination.  

Ultimately we are arriving at a sort of scalene triangle relation between the three factors, where prioritizing one, leads to an inconvenience in the second factor, and a complete sacrifice of the third factor.
<br><br>


## **Superfactors**
I have also realized two important "superfactors" as I call them, that also have an overall effect on your commute, but this effect is equally distributed across the Time, Cost and Comfort factors. If the side lengths of the scalene triangle represents the three factors of commuting, think of these superfactors as scale factor that the sides are multiplied by. It is a weird analogy, but I can't think of anything better, so we'll stick with that.
<br><br>

## **Where you Live?**
The first superfactor is the place you live. If you dwell in the centre of the city and are well connected to all areas as a result of that, then more likely than not, you don't need to travel a long distance to reach your destination daily. This is because most workplaces are generally in the central city areas anyways. So essentially, minimizing the commute distance, has a positive effect on Time, Cost and Comfort of commuting. 

Unfortunately, this is becoming more difficult, as housing in the centre of any city is very rarely found, and even if found, is extremely expensive. Most people prefer to live more comfortably and for less money, on the outskirts of the city or in satellite towns aroud the city. 
<br><br>

## **When you Travel?**
Another superfactor is the time of the day you choose to commute. If your commute needs to happen during the times when most of the people are also commuting, say peak rush hours of the morning or evening, then that negatively affects the Time, Cost and Comfort of your commute. 

If you are lucky enough to have a flexible enough schedule to alter your commute by a few hours before or after the time when the general public travels, you'll find a hugely positive impact across all three factors.
<br><br>

## **Conclusion**
The need for commuting is somewhat reduced over the past few decades, due to increased access to easy digital communication. Some industries have shifted to a completely online, work-from-home policy, while others are moving towards at least a hybrid approach. But still, it cannot be denied that most of the world works and studies in-person, and commute is still a major part of many people's day. 

Based on the factors I identified, I am coming to the realization that unless I am lucky enough that my superfactors make my commute easy, there has to be sacrifices made, in order to get to where I need to go. It is high time we fixed this with proper and more importantly, scalable solution to transport people in masses. But even such solutions fail eventually with rise in commuting population.

The more permanent method would be to de-urbanize and distribute ourselves into self sustaining satellite towns, and work remotely. Though that is the healthiest and most peaceful option, right now it remains only a luxury for a few people, as the totally remote work culture has not caught on completely and some occupations require physical involvement.

I guess I end this with the hope that, at least whatever work can go fully remote, will eventually go remote and that will free up the resources of the public transport to the people who really need it, in order to commute quickly and effectively.

You can catch me on my mailbox and let me know what y'all think about this. Is this rantanalysis consistent with your approach? Or am I an idiot overanalysing the mundaneness of my life. Whatever be the case, I continue to dream of the day when the problem of commuting is solved.
